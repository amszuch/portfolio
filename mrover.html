Individual Contribution

When joining MRover, my particular area of interest was the inverse-kinematics (IK) control system for the six-degree-of-freedom robotic arm. Throughout the Fall 2021 semester, I worked closely with Cameron Tressler, Sam Korman, and Bryan Li on developing, debugging, and refining our IK capabilities and path-planning. In the beginning of the Winter 2022 semester, Cam and I spent countless hours at the Wilson Center, integrating our code with the embedded software and robotic arm teams. As the weather broke, I transitioned more towards GUI refinement ahead of the summer competition season and attended Mars Yard testing. To see some of the code I contributed, check out the ra_kinematics directory and pull request #1038 in the archived Github repo.

Although I was unable to travel and compete with the team, much of the work I did contributed directly to the success of the SAR video (see below), between filming robotic arm capabilities and fine tuning the specific kinematics example shown. I also helped our team adapt the existing codebase to function on the M1 Mac (more out of necessity than passion!), which prompted the team to transfer to Robot Operating System (ROS) after the 2021-2022 academic year.

Team Successes

The Michigan Mars Rover Team performed very well at the University Rover Challenge (URC), winning first place with an overall score of 388.97 out of 500 possible points. You can check out Rosie and the new capabilities introduced in 2022 in the System Acceptance Review (SAR) video below, through which the team scored 90.97 of 100 possible points.


While URC represents the most competitive environment for university rover competition and guides much of the design for the rover each year, MRover also competed at the 2022 Canadian International Rover Competition (CIRC), also winning first place. This was a new competition for the team, and an excellent opportunity for younger members to travel despite holding smaller roles on the team.
